RESEARCH

Evolutionary History of Methanogenesis and Archaea

The domain Archaea was originally discovered as specialists in lithoautotrophic methanogenesis and are now recognized to take a plethora of lifestyles including organotrophy, parasitism, and symbiosis that led to the emergence of Eukarya. To uncover what ways of life ancient archaea took and how this gave rise to physiologies prominent today (i.e., methanogenesis and symbiosis), we bring milestones of early archaeal evolution into focus through holistic exploration of genetically traceable (genes) and untraceable (extant physiologies) features related to lithoautotrophy. Phylogenetic and experimental evidence suggests that methanogenesis by Archaea originated from non-enzymatic methane-producing reactions catalyzed by the key coenzyme F430 and, after innovation of the central enzyme of methanogenesis (i.e., methyl-S-CoM reductase), the last Archaea common ancestor emerged as a versatile H2-, CO2-, and methanol-utilizing methanogen. Subsequently, stepwise specialization of methanogenesis diversified methanogens and, in parallel, drove loss of lithotrophy, autotrophy, and thermophily in two major archaeal lineages – DPANN and Asgardarchaeota – paving way for transition towards slow-growing energetically-dependent symbiosis conducive for eukaryogenesis. Thus, methanogenesis is far more than a hallmark metabolism of Archaea, but the evolutionary fulcrum at the domain’s foundation.
