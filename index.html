I am now a postdoc working with Dr. Masaru K. NOBU at the National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology, Japan (AIST, 産業技術総合研究所) in the Microbial and Genetic Resources Research Group. My current research topic is revealing the evolutionary history of microorganisms inhabiting natural and engineered ecosystems, with a particular focus on archaea, one of the most primitive forms of life. I hold a Ph.D. (2020) and an M.S. (2015), both in Environmental Science and Engineering, from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (UIUC), and a B.S. in Energy and Resource Engineering (2013) from Peking University, China.

Research Topics

 

History of Archaea: from methane to complexity



Archaea is one of the two most primitive domains of life, alongside Bacteria, that originated and prospered on ancient Earth. Archaea was originally believed to be methanogenic specialists or extremophiles but now found to be diverse. Furthermore, some of them are proposed to have played a key role in eukaryogenesis, a milestone evolutionary event that eventually gave rise to the glorious colour eukaryotic lives manifest today. To illuminate how the transformation took place, I am currently working closely, i.e., sitting next to and having lunch together everyday, with Dr. Nobu by leveraging the increasingly available archaeal genome repository, developing computation pipelines, and performing careful phylogenetic analyses. We believe our results could depict some fascinating stories that happened millions years ago.

 
 

Uncharted majority: metabolism of anaerobes



Anaerobic environments, e.g., sediment, subsurface, bioreactor, and intestinal tract, harbor tremendous uncharted microbial diversity, which are closely related to natural nutrient cycle, waste remediation, and human health. Meta-omics have been demonstrated as effective approaches to tease apart the metabolic complexity in anaerobic environments and reveal the ecological functions of microbial dark matter. In my research, I leveraged on cutting-edge bioinformatic tools to capture anaerobic lineages that carry significant ecological relevance, including those participating in direct interspecies electron transfer, natural and anthropogenic polymer degradation, and anaerobic respiration. From these studies, I've cultivated my analytical skills and enriched my knowledge, which could be applied to the vast diversity of uncharted anaerobes.

 
 

In a reactor: microbial composition and treatment performance



Characteristics of different types of waste and wastewater varies significantly, and, thus, specific engineering of microbial composition in bioreactors is necessary. I have applied a set of ecological approaches, including community survey, meta-omics, FISH, and statistics, to explored the link between microbial composition and treatment performance in various full-scale and lab-scale bioreactors, including anaerobic digesters, activated sludge, packed-bed reactors, UASB reactors, sequential batch reactors, DHS reactors, and anaerobic fermenters. I believe, accurate decoding of microbial composition is critical for achieving superior treatment performance, and elaborate design and operation of bioreactors could in turn serve as an effective approach to uncover novel microbial processes.

 
 

Between communities: impacts of immigration



Microbial ecosystems are usually physically connected with each other, allowing microorganisms to travel between communities, i.e., microbial immigration. My works are among the first attempts that explicitly quantify immigration's impacts on community assembly and function. Using wastewater treatment processes as the model system and an ecogenomics-based mass balance approach, it is demonstrated that immigration's impacts could vary considerably, depending on the flux intensity, the physiological characteristic of immigrants, and the physicochemical features of up- and downstream environments. Rethinking community ecology as a continuum, the concept and methodology of my works are adapted by increasing number of studies to renovate how we decipher community assembly processes and how we correlate microbial ecology and treatment performance.
