Research Topics

 

Evolution and comparative genomics: methanogenesis and Archaea



Archaea represent one of the two most primitive forms of life that originated and prospered on ancient Earth. Archaea were originally believed to be methanogenic specialists or extremophiles, but are now found to inhabit a plethora of modern ecosystems with diverse lifestyles. Furthermore, some archaea might have played a key role in eukaryogenesis, a milestone evolutionary event that gave rise to the eukaryotic lives. To illuminate the evolutionary trajectory, I am leveraging the increasingly available archaeal genome repository, developing computation pipelines, and performing in-depth phylogenetic analyses. Our results depict some fascinating events that took place millions of years ago and exerted tremendous influences on modern lifes on Earth.

Related studies: evolutionary history of methanogenesis [Ref 1 in publication list]; global gene catalog (in preparation).

 
 

Environmental microbiology: ecophysiology of uncultured anaerobes



Anaerobic environments, e.g., sediment, subsurface, bioreactor, and intestinal tract, harbor tremendous uncharted microbial diversity that are involved in natural nutrient cycle, waste remediation, and human health. Meta-omics could effectively teasing apart the metabolic complexity in anaerobic environments and revealing the ecological functions of microbial dark matters. In my research, I employed cutting-edge bioinformatic tools to capture anaerobic lineages that carry significant ecological relevance, including those participating in direct interspecies electron transfer, natural and anthropogenic polymer degradation, and anaerobic respiration.

Related studies: eco-physiology of Bacteroidales [3] and Geobacter [9]; community structure of anaerobic consortia degrading amino acids [4] and benzoate [12].

 
 

Environmental engineering: biological wastewater treatment



The characteristics of wastewater varies significantly, and, thus, bioreactors engineered to harbor unique microbial composition is necessary. I have applied a set of ecological approaches, including community survey, meta-omics, FISH, and statistics, to explored the link between microbial composition and treatment performance in full-scale and lab-scale bioreactors (anaerobic digesters, activated sludge, packed-bed reactors, UASB reactors, sequential batch reactors, DHS reactors, etc.). Precisely decoding microbial composition is critical for achieving superior treatment performance. Elaborate design of bioreactors could in turn serve as an effective approach to uncover novel microbial processes.

Related studies: lab-scale bioreactors treating softdrink wastewater [9], full-scale anaerobic digesters treating sewage sludge [5, 10], and full-scale anaerobic-aerobic porcess treating petrochemical wastewater [8].

 
 

Community ecology: impacts of microbial immigration



Microbial ecosystems are usually physically connected, allowing microorganisms to travel between communities, i.e. , microbial immigration. By applying a set of ecological tools, my work revealed that the impacts of microbial immigration varied considerably, depending on the flux intensity, the physiological characteristic of immigrating microbes, and the physicochemical features of upstream and downstream environments. Rethinking community ecology as a continuum, the concept and methodology could be applied to diverse ecosystems to renovate how we decipher community assembly processes and how we correlate microbial ecology and community function.

Related studies: characterize microbial immigration using meta-omic approaches [2], a mechine learning method [8], in full-scale anaerobic digesters [11], and in lab-scale fermenters [6]; a review article [7].
